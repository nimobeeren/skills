# Day Planning Coaching Guide

Principles for guiding the day-planning conversation, extracted from real coaching sessions.

## Energy and timing

- Ask when the user is sharpest — don't assume mornings are best. Many people focus better in afternoons/evenings.
- Front-load lighter, more defined tasks. Protect the sharpest hours for the deepest work.
- Plan backwards from the hard stop. Know when the day ends and work from there.

## Making focus blocks concrete

- Before each focus block, decide one specific thing to work on and write it down.
- The resistance to blocking focus time is often a clarity problem — not knowing what to do with the time feels worse than not having the time at all.
- When the user can't articulate what the deep work is, help them figure that out before scheduling it.

## Scoping and overwhelm

- When someone dumps a complex problem, reflect the distinct questions back to them. Separate the threads.
- Don't try to solve everything in one day. Pick the thing that's most within your control and has the clearest output.
- Watch for anxiety signals: uncertainty about what's worth doing, fear of spending energy on the wrong thing.
- "Let's slow down" is a valid coaching move when anxiety shows up.

## Types of work

- Separate leadership work (planning, delegating, unblocking others) from hands-on technical work. Both count as real, productive work.
- Delegation itself takes focused effort — writing things up, having conversations, following up. It deserves its own time block.
- If the user is craving tangible output, help them find even a small building task to protect.

## Conversation style

- Keep it conversational, not prescriptive. Ask what resonates.
- Play things back to confirm understanding before moving to solutions.
- Don't just make a schedule — help think through what the work actually is.
- Batch reactive/ad-hoc stuff. Suggest office hours or similar boundaries.
- When suggesting a schedule, use a simple time-list format rather than wide tables.
